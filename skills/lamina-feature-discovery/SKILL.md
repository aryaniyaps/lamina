# Feature Discovery

## Decision frameworks

- **The common UX research failure modes**: Credulity, Dogmatism, Bias, Obscurantism, Laziness, Vagueness, Hubris. - When to use: Auditing research quality before and after studies. - How: Credulity = asking what users want; Dogmatism = one "right" method; Bias = cherry-picking; Obscurantism = hoarding findings; Laziness/Vagueness/Hubris = shortcuts and overconfidence.
- **Think Like a Detective**: Excavate fragile truth carefully; what people say → what they do. - When to use: Planning any user contact. - How: Observe current behavior or prototype use; avoid direct preference/prediction questions.
- **The Two Questions We Answer with UX Research**: (1) Are we building the right thing? (2) Are we building the thing right? - When to use: Scoping whether research is generative/discovery or evaluative/validation. - How: Right thing = needs, goals, opportunities; thing right = usability, fit, satisfaction with solution.
- **Anatomy of a Research Question**: Problem statement sharpened into falsifiable, actionable inquiry. - When to use: Before choosing methods or participants. - How: Define business need, user need, and decision the research will inform.
- **Applying Psychology to UX Research**: Prospect Theory, confabulation, unreliable introspection. - When to use: Interpreting stated preferences and survey data. - How: Trust behavior over self-report; design studies that reveal mental processes indirectly.
- **Research by Purpose**(four types): - **Generative/exploratory**— "What's up with...?" Before you know the problem; interviews, field observation, literature review. - **Descriptive/explanatory**— "What and how?" Homework on a defined problem; same activities, different high-level question. - **Evaluative**— "Are we getting close?" Test proposed solutions iteratively; usability testing is the most common form. - **Causal**— "Why is this happening?" Post-launch; analytics and multivariate testing to establish cause-and-effect.
- **Research Roles**(task clusters, not people): Author, Interviewer/Moderator, Coordinator/Scheduler, Notetaker/Recorder, Recruiter, Analyst, Documenter, Observer.
- **Objection Responses**: Pre-built answers to "we don't have time/budget/expertise/CEO dictates anyway."
- **Jeff Patton's Agile UX Practices**(three points): Aggressively prioritize highest-value users; analyze and model data quickly and collaboratively; defer less urgent research while software is constructed.
- **Bias Checklist**: Design bias, sampling bias, interviewer bias, sponsor bias, social desirability bias, Hawthorne effect.
- **Best Practices Checklist**(Franklin method): (1) Phrase questions clearly, (2) Set realistic expectations, (3) Be prepared, (4) Allow time for analysis, (5) Take dictation.
- **Known Unknowns / Risk Prioritization**: Ask what bad things happen in six months if key assumptions are wrong; skip research only when no real-world user risk exists.
- **The Satisfying Click**: Patterns emerge from data; collaborate to avoid seeing patterns you want; keep asking until pieces fall into place.
- **The Four Ds of Design Ethnography**:
- **Deep dive**: Small number of representative users, known very well—not thousand-person surveys.
- **Daily life**: Get into the messy field; participant observation in actual environments.
- **Data analysis**: Systematic sifting distinguishes ethnography from networking.
- **Drama!**: Lively narratives → personas and scenarios that keep teams honest.
- **Three-Box Interview Structure**: Introduction/warm-up → body (open questions) → conclusion (wrap-up, incentives).
- **Interview Guide Components**: Study goal, demographic context questions, icebreakers, primary focus questions.
- **Contextual Inquiry**("consensual home invasion"): Enter participant's environment; observe activities in situ; summarize and verify observations.
- **Follow Me Home**(Scott Cook/Intuit): Observe software setup and use in purchaser's actual home.
- **Helsinki Design Lab Ethnography Checklist**: Welcoming atmosphere, listen more than speak, natural context, open questions, photos, exact vocabulary, note post-recording revelations.
- **Everything in Context**(four layers): Physical environment, mental model, habits, relationships.

## Checklists

1. What people say is not as useful as what people do—people are unreliable witnesses.
2. Answer "right thing?" with discovery research; "thing right?" with evaluative research.
3. Fight common UX research failure modes—especially credulity and dogmatism.
4. Triangulate methods: qualitative explains why; quantitative confirms what at scale.
5. Ensure everyone on the team gets exposure hours—not just the UX researcher.
6. Sharpen vague briefs into research questions that end with a decision, not a report.
1. Involve the whole team from the start—shared discovery beats stunning reports nobody reads.
2. Classify research by the question you're asking, not academic taxonomy.
3. Evaluative research should be ongoing, not one-shot.
4. Pre-answer common objections before stakeholders raise them.
5. Listen, be interested, ask questions, write clearly, practice.
1. Shut up and listen—good interviewing is harder than good talking.
2. Conduct research in users' natural context; control is the enemy of insight.
3. Use open questions, embrace silence, probe with "tell me more about that."
4. Capture exact vocabulary for interface language.
5. A few phone calls can change everything—accept no substitute for real people.

## Heuristics

- **Actionable and Testable Insights**: Research succeeds when findings drive decisions and experiments.
- **Confabulation**: Users invent plausible reasons for choices they can't actually explain.
- **Position Effect**: Preference driven by layout, not quality (Nisbett & Wilson stocking study).
- **Triangulation**: Combining qualitative and quantitative methods on the same phenomenon.
- **Iterative Design Limits**: Iteration optimizes solutions but doesn't discover unmet needs alone.
- **Exposure Hours**: Team members observing users regularly (Spool: 2 hours per 6 weeks).
- Use observation over interrogation when you need truth about behavior—"excavate delicately."
- Think right thing vs. thing right before picking field study vs. usability test.
- Treat surveys as validation tools after you know the right questions—not discovery hammers.
- Research is a team sport—findings trapped in one researcher's head fail the organization.
- **Decision-driven research**: Every project is a series of decisions; include only research supporting anticipated decisions.
- **Collaborative research**: People who collect insights look for opportunities to apply them—"obeying the smart person" kills adoption.
- **Research lead**: One person keeps protocol and quality; others contribute domain expertise.
- **Just enough rigor**: Note bias, use checklists, maintain discipline—rigorous, not rigid.
- **Ethics starter set**: Project ethics, method ethics, informed consent, safety/privacy (never interview anyone driving).
- **How much is enough**: No fixed answer—stop when sufficiently informed; listen for the click.
- Research as**toolbox**: Find the right tool fast; don't obsess over academic classification.
- **Ensemble, not solo star**: Developers notice tech habits, content strategists catch vocabulary, visual designers spot cultural signals.
- **Falling back on ignorance**(agency advantage): Naive questions like "How does that benefit the business?" cut through assumptions.
- **Customer service as data trove**: Where human needs crash into reality—in-house teams should mine support tickets and talk to reps.
- **Organizational terrain**: Startups = islands; corporations = Australia—same rules, different navigation difficulty.
- **Ethnography**: Qualitative methods to understand a cultural group's ordinary activities in habitual environments—"What do people do and why?"
- **First rule of user research**: Never ask anyone what they want.
- **"Everybody lies"**(House M.D. maxim): People are poor reporters of preferences; even honest people lack self-knowledge for speculative scenarios.
- **Indirect questions**: The questions you ask and the answers you need are different sets—get stories, not self-assessments.
- **Assumptions are insults**: Wrong embedded assumptions alienate users and can build discrimination into products.
- **Focus groups: just say no**: Research theater—artificial environment, social desirability, performance not behavior; Merton intended them only as idea sources needing further research.
- **Semi-structured = flexibility**: Prepared topics, responsive follow-up.
- **Interviewer as host and student**: Put participant at ease, then become nearly invisible—world's foremost expert on themselves.
- **Break into their brain, not their house**: Front-door direct questions hit defenses; observation and oblique questions get through.
- **SimCity → NYC resource allocation**: Ideal scenarios designed at desks don't reflect messy reality.
- **Insert new hook into existing habit**: Changing habits is hard; piggybacking on current behavior is easier.
- **Intuitive = matches mental model**: Novel interfaces without hooks force learning from scratch.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Research Type Selector
- **When**: You need to pick what kind of inquiry fits the decision at hand.
- **Process**: Generative when problem undefined; descriptive when context of known problem needed; evaluative when testing solutions; causal when explaining post-launch behavior.
- **Pass**: Method matches the question form: what's up / what and how / are we close / why is this happening.
- **Failure signals**: Using surveys for discovery; using interviews for statistical validation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Asking users what they want**: Produces confabulated, unreliable answers.
- **Method-led research**: Doing surveys because you have survey tools.
- **Cherry-picking results**: Hiding data that threatens product assumptions.
- **Solo researcher as sole user expert**: Delegating all user understanding to one person.
- **Vague research without a question mark**: "Do some research on X" without decision target.
- **Researcher/designer split**: Designers as passive recipients of analysis rather than co-investigators.
- **Interviewer as notetaker**: One person doing both degrades session quality.
- **Methodology wars**(qual vs quant): The question determines the method.
- **"Just tossing in research"**(freelancers): Include research in fees and scope, don't absorb it unpaid.
- **Beta as substitute**: Beta finds broken features, not whether people want the product or you're solving the right problem.
- **Root objection = laziness + fear**: Especially fear of being wrong in "rockstar knows best" cultures.
- **Asking "what do you want?"**: Gets socially desirable, self-flattering, imagination-limited answers.
- **Focus groups as qualitative research**: Antithesis of ethnography; one bad recruit tanks the whole session.
- **Conference room interviews**: No one acts naturally; even phone from their home/office is better than your room.
- **Talking about yourself**: Active listening becomes "let me tell you about my similar experience."
- **Leading and closed questions**: "Do you communicate with marketing often?" vs "Tell me about the internal groups you communicate with."
- **Designing for yourself**: Age, gender, ability assumptions create unintended barriers.

## Examples

- **What Is Ux Research**: A team asks usability participants "Which of these three designs do you prefer?"—mirroring the Nisbett & Wilson stocking study where identical items ranked differently by position. Participants confabulate reasons (cleaner layout, better colors). & redirect: observe participants completing real tasks with prototypes; note where they hesitate, succeed, or work around the UI. The research question becomes "Can users complete account setup in under 3 minutes without assistance?"—actionable, testable, and immune to preference theater.
- **Research Type Basics**: Fantastic Science Center sees rising mobile traffic but doesn't know what to build. Organizational research reveals which interactions benefit the museum; user research finds school districts prohibit phones on field trips, but parents photograph family visits and share on Facebook. Conclusion: target parents with social media campaigns, not students with in-museum phone apps. Shows generative + descriptive research combining for a concrete decision.
- **User Research Methods**: Parent interview for Fantastic Science Center: "Walk me through a typical week" reveals Saturday is family time, partner gets break while other parent handles kids, checks email on phone before getting out of bed, sees event banners while driving but forgets them. Contextual inquiry at home shows four-year-old interruptions, texts partner to coordinate activities, relies on iPhone alarm for school pickup. Analysis yields persona Diane—pressed for time, multi-device, needs low-effort planning support and reminders, not more complexity.
