# Interview Design

## Decision frameworks

- **Question Types and Techniques**: Open vs. closed; past behavior vs. hypothetical future; "tell me about" vs. "do you ever." - Use when writing interview guides. - How: Prefer past-behavior and story prompts over hypotheticals and yes/no.
- **Phrasing Impact**: Same intent, different words yield different depth. - Use when a question falls flat. - How: Rephrase from "Do you like X?" to "Tell me about the last time you used X."
- **Silence as Technique**: Pausing after answers invites elaboration. - Use when answers feel thin or rehearsed. - How: Count to three; resist filling silence; nod and wait.
- **Avoiding Leading Questions**: Embedded assumptions that steer answers. - Use during guide review and live sessions. - How: Audit for "don't you think" and "how much do you love"; neutralize.
- **Follow-Up Probes**: "Tell me more," "What happened next," "Why was that?" - Use when something tweaks your antenna. - How: Drill down on surprising words, emotions, or hesitations.
- **Follow-Up Probes**: "Tell me more," "What happened next," "What made that difficult?" drill into thin answers. - Use whenever something surprises or contradicts assumptions. - How: Park scripted topics temporarily; follow the thread; return to guide when exhausted.
- **Best Practices for Interviewing (overall approach)**: Curiosity over certainty; participant as expert; researcher as learner. - Use as foundation for every session. - How: Adopt beginner's mind; suppress solution defense; follow interesting threads.
- **Check Your Worldview at the Door**: Suspend product attachment and organizational assumptions during sessions. - Use especially when testing your own concepts. - How: Notice when you're defending; redirect to their experience.
- **Embrace How Someone Else Sees the World**: Empathic stance without agreeing or arguing. - Use throughout interviews. - How: Reflect back; ask clarifying questions; never correct their experience.
- **Listen More Than You Talk**: Ratio heavily favoring participant speech. - Use as continuous self-monitor. - How: Count your interruptions; use silence; ask open follow-ups.
- **Be Present**: Full attention—not planning the next question while they answer. - Use in every moment of the session. - How: Put away devices not needed for capture; note follow-ups for later.
- **Interviewing vs. Chatting**: Deliberate choices about what to say, when, and when to say nothing distinguish research from conversation. - Use as self-check during sessions. - How: Notice when you're performing social rapport vs. collecting behavioral data.
- **Common Interview Variations**: In-person contextual, remote video, dyads/couples, expert/specialist, stakeholder interviews. - Use when selecting format for research questions. - How: Match variation to context needed; adapt logistics and guide accordingly.
- **Typical Challenges**: Dominant participants, quiet participants, tangents, group dynamics, technical issues. - Use when sessions go off-plan. - How: Redirect gently; address quiet members directly; park tangents for later.
- **Remote Interviewing Adaptations**: Camera framing, screen share, recording consent, connection backup plans. - Use for distributed research. - How: Test tech beforehand; ask for environment tour via camera; share screens for concept tests.
- **Improving as an Interviewer**: Record and review sessions; seek peer critique; practice stakeholder vs. user differences. - Use continuously across career. - How: Monthly session review; note every leading question you asked; iterate guide.
- **Interviewing Stakeholders vs. Users**: Stakeholders need alignment on objectives; harder to ask "dumb" questions when credibility feels at stake. - Use during planning phase. - How: Same techniques but explicit permission-seeking for naive questions.
- **Remote Interviewing Adaptations**: Environment tour via camera, screen share for concepts, phone backup when video fails. - Use for all remote sessions. - How: First five minutes confirm tech; ask participant to show workspace; test recording levels.

## Checklists

1. Question phrasing, timing, and silence dramatically affect answer quality.
2. Prefer past-behavior and story prompts over hypotheticals and likability questions.
3. Silence is a deliberate technique—not awkward gap to fill.
4. Audit guides for leading and double-barreled questions before fieldwork.
5. Follow surprising moments with probes, not the next scripted topic.

3. Listen more than you talk; silence is a powerful probe.

## Heuristics

- **Memory vs. reconstruction**: Past-behavior questions access memory; hypotheticals access imagination.
- **"Why" carefully**: Can sound accusatory; "What led you to…" often softer.
- **Double-barreled questions**: Two questions in one—split them.
- **Silence is not awkward**: It's invitation.
- **Word choices matter**: "Problem" vs. "challenge" vs. "situation" frame responses.
- Questions are**lenses**—each phrasing reveals a different facet.
- Leading questions are**self-fulfilling prophecies**in research clothing.
- Thin answers often mean**wrong question shape**, not uncooperative participant.
- **Decision filter**: Ask whether this finding changes a specific design or business decision—if not, dig deeper or stop.
- **Interviewing vs. chatting**: Deliberate choices about what to say, when, and when to say nothing.
- **Natural style, deliberate technique**: Leverage your personality but make specific tactical choices.
- **Participant as expert of their experience**: You are not there to educate them about the product.
- **Years of practice**: Skilled interviewing is hard and develops over time.
- **Tactical practices emerge from framework**: Word choice, silence, and pacing follow from stance.
- Interviewer is**guest in their world**, not**inspector of your product**.
- Every leading question is**a hypothesis smuggled into data collection**.
- Presence is**respect made audible**.
- **Dyad dynamics**: Couples or colleagues influence each other—observe both, manage dominance.
- **Remote is still contextual**: Ask for tour of workspace via webcam.
- **Improvement is iterative**: Years of deliberate practice, not one training course.
- **Stakeholder interviews**: 6–8 conversations aligning business and research objectives.
- **Challenge ≠ failure**: Difficult sessions often yield richest data if managed well.
- Format is**context instrument**—choose variation that matches research question.
- Session review is**game tape**for interviewers.
- Every dominant participant has a**quiet counterpart worth hearing**.

## Prompts

- "Tell me about the last time you..."
- "Walk me through the last time you..."
- "What happened next?"
- "What made that difficult?"
- "What led you to..." (prefer over accusatory "why")

## Anti-patterns

- **"Would you use this?"**: Hypothetical future preference—unreliable data.
- **"Don't you find X frustrating?"**: Leading toward agreement.
- **Survey questions in interviews**: Likert scales aloud instead of conversation.
- **Fear of silence**: Interrupting before participant finishes thinking.
- **Pitching during interviews**: Explaining why the product is good.
- **Correcting participants**: "Actually, that feature does X" mid-session.
- **Interviewing to confirm**: Only hearing what you already believe.
- **Multi-tasking facilitation**: Checking phone, thinking about lunch, planning debrief aloud.
- **Same guide for all variations**: Stakeholder questions pasted into user sessions.
- **Ignoring group dynamics**: Letting one voice speak for everyone in dyads.
- **No remote backup plan**: Lost session when WiFi fails without phone fallback.
- **Avoiding review**: Never listening to your own recordings.

## Examples

- **Asking Questions**: Research objective: understand digital music playlist management. Weak: "Do you update playlists often?" Strong: "Walk me through the last time you updated a playlist—what triggered it, what did you do first?" Participant reveals they only update before road trips—a pattern the yes/no question would miss entirely.
