# Research Scoping

## Decision frameworks

- **right-sized research scoping**: Match research scope to the specific decisions you need to make. - Use when assumptions outpace evidence or stakeholders resist inquiry. - How: Identify key decisions → select matching research → act on findings → keep asking questions.
- **Context Is Everything**(Segway lesson): Where humans are concerned, context is everything—products fail when they ignore existing systems, conventions, and needs. - Use when evaluating new features, platforms, or interaction paradigms. - How: Map how people currently behave within their environment before proposing change.
- **Design Research**(Jane Fulton Suri): Research integral to designing—exposes behavioral patterns, explores reactions to probes and prototypes, sheds light on the unknown through iterative hypothesis and experiment.

## Checklists

1. Research determines whether you're solving the right problem, not just building something.
2. Match research investment to decision risk—small questions need small studies.
3. Admitting uncertainty early costs less than discovering wrong assumptions after launch.
4. Context (social systems, conventions, constraints) kills products that ignore it.
5. A skeptical, question-driven culture is more valuable than any single methodology.

## Heuristics

- **Pure research**: Creates new human knowledge; rigorous, peer-reviewed, observation/experiment-based.
- **Applied research**: Borrows from pure research for a real-world goal; methods can be relaxed; success = contribution to stated goal.
- **Design research**: Understanding the people for whom we design; core to user-centered design (distinct from academic "study of design").
- **Systematic inquiry**: Research is structured critical thinking, not a Google query and done.
- **What research is not**: Not asking what people "like"; not a political tool; not science (avoid statistical-significance arguments with sample-size queens).
- Research as**periscope**: Better view of surroundings without stopping forward motion.
- **Skeptical mindset**as the real deliverable: Answers breed more questions; that habit beats any single methodology.
- **"I don't know"**as professional strength: The creative-genius myth makes admitting uncertainty feel dangerous.
- **Fantastic Science Center**: Recurring case study—a museum with a vague web-improvement grant needs research to prioritize alternatives.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Right Sized Research Scope
- **When**: Any project where decisions depend on assumptions that have not been tested.
- **Process**: Identify key decisions  ->  select research type (generative/descriptive/evaluative/causal)  ->  assign roles  ->  execute  ->  document findings  ->  act on results.
- **Pass**: Each major assumption maps to evidence or an explicit accepted risk.
- **Failure signals**: Scope too narrow  ->  missed edge cases; scope too wide  ->  delayed decisions.

### Research Type Selector
- **When**: You need to pick what kind of inquiry fits the decision at hand.
- **Process**: Generative when problem undefined; descriptive when context of known problem needed; evaluative when testing solutions; causal when explaining post-launch behavior.
- **Pass**: Method matches the question form: what's up / what and how / are we close / why is this happening.
- **Failure signals**: Using surveys for discovery; using interviews for statistical validation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Blue-sky without grounding**: Refusing to talk to salespeople because "we don't want the present to inhibit creativity" produces irresponsible speculation.
- **Likability as metric**: "Like" is subjective, self-reported, and unmoored from behavior—quash liking and hating in critical analysis.
- **Research theater**: Usability labs or methods chosen to appear "research-y" rather than answer the question.
- **Science cosplay**: Arguing statistical significance for applied qualitative work instead of focusing on useful insights.

## Examples

- **Enough Research Scope**: An insurance company hires Mule Design to identify product opportunities from emerging personal technologies. When the firm asks to interview salespeople and agents, the client refuses—fearing current practices will limit "blue-sky thinking." The team does secondary research only. The work is solid but weaker because it lacks the client's specific operational context. lesson: innovation requires knowing the current state and why things are the way they are.
