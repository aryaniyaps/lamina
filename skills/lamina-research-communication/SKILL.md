## Decision frameworks

- **Evangelizing UX Research**: Five alternatives to wordy reports—journey maps, photo-ethnographies, affinity diagramming, screenshot forensics, hallway evangelism. - Use when findings aren't reaching decision-makers. - How: Match format to audience attention span and decision timeline.
- **How to Create a User Journey Map**: Visual narrative of user experience across touchpoints. - Use when stakeholders need holistic view of pain points. - How: Stages, actions, thoughts, emotions, opportunities—co-created with team.
- **Generating Solutions to Usability Problems**: Research findings → structured ideation workshops. - Use after usability testing reveals problems. - How: Present findings visually; facilitate solution brainstorming tied to observed behaviors.
- **Building UX Research Into the Design Studio Methodology**: Integrate research readouts into design studio sessions. - Use in agile/design sprint contexts. - How: Brief evidence presentation → sketch → critique cycle grounded in data.
- **Dealing with Common Objections to UX Research**: Pre-built responses to time, budget, and "we already know" objections. - Use when research is blocked or dismissed. - How: Reframe as risk reduction; cite cost of wrong assumptions.
- **The User Experience Debrief Meeting**: Structured meeting format for presenting findings with engagement. - Use after every significant study. - How: Show video clips, prioritize issues live, assign owners before meeting ends.
- **Creating a User Experience Dashboard**: Ongoing metrics visible to stakeholders. - Use for sustained influence beyond single projects. - How: Task success, satisfaction, support tickets—trended over time.
- **Achieving Boardroom Influence**: Executive-level communication of UX ROI. - Use when strategic decisions ignore user evidence. - How: Business language, conversion/support metrics, competitive risk framing.
- **Creating a User Experience Dashboard**: Trend task success, satisfaction, and support tickets for sustained executive visibility. - Use after initial project debriefs. - How: Pick 3–5 metrics aligned to key tasks; update weekly; review in existing ceremonies.
- **Aligning Business and Research Objectives**: research question alignment session and stakeholder involvement during planning. - Use at project kickoff. - How: Co-define business problem and research questions; reprioritize together.
- **Engaging Colleagues in Interviews**: Participation, debriefing, and highlight review for team ownership. - Use during fieldwork and analysis. - How: Invite observers; conduct live debriefs; share video clips.
- **Organizational Research Infrastructure**: Baseline knowledge assembled over time feeding all product development. - Use in mature research practices. - How: Repository of findings; searchable insights; onboarding new team members to existing knowledge.
- **Scaling Interviewing Across the Organization**: Research leaders train "people who do research" (PwDR) while maintaining quality standards. - Use when demand exceeds specialist capacity. - How: Training programs; review protocols; specialist oversight of distributed research.
- **Making Impact When Time Is Constrained**: Trade-offs for lean timelines—focused questions, smaller samples, iterative rounds. - Use under deadline pressure. - How: Ruthlessly prioritize research questions; integrate findings into existing ceremonies.
- **Scaling Interviewing (PwDR)**: Train people who do research while specialists maintain guide review and quality standards. - Use when demand exceeds specialist capacity. - How: Certification on consent and question craft; sample review of recordings; shared repository.

## Heuristics

- **Research impact infrastructure**: Process and formats that embed findings in decisions.
- **Face validity**: Methods stakeholders can observe and trust (usability tests, journey maps).
- **Screenshot forensics**: Annotated screenshots walking stakeholders through observed failures.
- **way evangelism**: Informal, persistent sharing of user insights.
- **Arrive early, engage continuously**: Don't wait until the final report.
- A unread report is**research that never happened**.
- Persuasion is**translation**—from user behavior to business language.
- Video clips create**empathy faster**than paragraphs. - **Decision filter**: Ask whether this finding changes a specific design or business decision—if not, dig deeper or stop.
- **Interviewing addresses a business need**: Research tied to decisions, not curiosity alone.
- **"We don't have time" often means "we don't need to"**: Challenge with vision-meets-reality framing.
- **Baseline body of knowledge**: Subsequent research enhances rather than starts from zero.
- **Everyone consumes results**: Even non-interviewers should access and use findings.
- **If stakeholders heard nothing new**: Check alignment, depth of analysis, and whether needs were already ignored.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Stakeholder Evidence Presentation
- **When**: Delivering findings to decision-makers.
- **Process**: Lead with business impact  ->  show video clips not quotes  ->  recommend specific actions  ->  anticipate objections  ->  offer stakeholder involvement in next phase.
- **Pass**: Decision-makers commit to specific next steps.
- **Failure signals**: Data dump; no recommendations; ignoring political context.

## Anti-patterns

- **The 40-page report**: Comprehensive but unread; arrives after decisions are made.
- **Research team as service bureau**: "Just tell me what to fix" without observation.
- **Data dumping**: Transcripts and quotes without prioritized recommendations.
- **One-shot evangelism**: Single presentation with no follow-through infrastructure.
- **One-shot studies without embedding**: Findings die after single presentation.
- **Research team as external vendor**: Internal teams never develop user empathy.
- **Skipping stakeholder alignment**: Perfect study answering wrong question.
- **Ignoring repeated findings**: Same user need reported for years with no product response.

## Examples

- **Persuading With Research**: Jill spends a week on an excellent field research report. The lead developer replies "Thanks, I'll get back to you"—and never does. reframes: she presents a 90-minute debrief with three video clips, a journey map on the wall, and a prioritized issue list with assigned owners. The team fixes two showstoppers before sprint end.
