# Problem Framing

## Decision frameworks

- **Qualitative versus Quantitative Data in Design Research**: Qualitative answers what, how, and why; quantitative answers how much along reductive axes. - Use when stakeholders demand numbers for behavioral questions. - How: Pair qualitative behavioral research with quantitative market sizing and analytics.
- **behavior-first design process Research**: Research techniques aligned to persona construction and scenario development. - Use when beginning any behavior-first project. - How: Interview and observe users; supplement with competitive/product audits and stakeholder interviews.
- **Interviewing and Observing Users**: Contextual inquiry combining observation with directed questions. - Use when you need behavioral detail in real environments. - How: Visit users in context; observe workflows; probe with open-ended questions.
- **Other Types of Qualitative Research**: Literature review, competitive/product audit, stakeholder interviews, ethnographic field study, user modeling workshops, usability testing.
- **Research Is Critical to Good Design**: Without behavioral knowledge, designers guess—and products fail in predictable ways. - Use when stakeholders question research ROI. - How: Tie research findings to specific go/no-go decisions and design requirements.
- **High-Level Research Plan**: Three elements—business problem, research questions, research method—plus stakeholder alignment before logistics. - When to use: At project kickoff, before recruiting or writing interview guides. - How: Document business objective, derive specific research questions, select method (interviewing when depth beats sample size), review with team.
- **Generative vs. Evaluative Research**: Generative discovers opportunities; evaluative tests what you're building. Organizations often deny they need generative research while already doing evaluative work. - When to use: When stakeholders say "we don't need research" or conflate usability testing with discovery. - How: Clarify whether you need to find new directions or validate existing designs.
- **Christian Rohrer's Research Landscape**: Methods organized by behavior/attitude and qualitative/quantitative axes. - When to use: Choosing among interviews, diary studies, surveys, usability testing, analytics. - How: Match method to goal—e.g., interviews and diary studies for finding new directions.
- **Sam Ladner's Lifecycle Guide**: Recommends research methods by product maturity/sales stage. - When to use: Planning research cadence across a product's life.

## Checklists

1. Outcomes are judged by meeting user needs and business goals—research grounds both.
2. Qualitative methods reveal behavioral patterns quantitative methods flatten.
3. behavior-first research feeds personas, scenarios, and requirements.
4. Quantitative data complements qualitative work for market sizing and problem detection.
5. Research delivers business insights beyond design—sometimes the right answer is don't build.
1. Articulate business problem and research question before participant questions.
2. Interviewing favors depth over statistical significance—use when you need rich, contextual understanding.
3. Satisficing means observed problems may not be problems users will pay effort to fix.
4. Reframing beats feature lists for innovation and risk identification.
5. Combine methods when you need both scale and depth.
6. Interview stakeholders (6–8) to surface assumptions, barriers, and objectives before fieldwork.

## Heuristics

- **Behavioral knowledge**: What users do, want, and struggle with—not just purchase intent.
- **Domain context**: Technical, business, and environmental constraints shaping design.
- **Market segmentation vs. user modeling**: Demographics predict buying; personas predict usage.
- **Research credibility**: Decisions traceable to findings give teams authority with management.
- Qualitative research is a**periscope**on the problem space—not a delay tactic.
- Market research opens the**business case**; design research opens the**product case**.
- Analytics tell you**where**users struggle; interviews tell you**why**.
- **Decision filter**: Ask whether this finding changes a specific design or business decision—if not, dig deeper or stop.
- **Satisficing**: Herbert Simon's term—people tolerate "good-enough" solutions; observed "pain points" may not motivate change.
- **Reframes**: Crucial perspective shifts from rigorous analysis that flip initial problems and reveal design opportunities.
- **Mixed methods**: Combining qualitative interviews with quantitative surveys, CLTs, or analytics in one study.
- **Rolling research / Continuous discovery**: Ongoing participant access vs. bounded project models.
- **Foraging model (anti-pattern)**: Treating fieldwork as collecting obvious needs without planning or interpretation.
- Think of interviews as**decision infrastructure**, not user appeasement—sometimes the right outcome is stopping a bad product (hard hat case).
- Use**satisficing**as a filter: before proposing a fix, ask whether the observed workaround costs less than your solution.
- Treat**reframes**as the highest-value research output, not feature request lists.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Research Question Alignment
- **When**: Before any interview or field study when stakeholders may have different expectations.
- **Process**: Gather team  ->  state business problem  ->  derive research questions  ->  surface assumptions  ->  agree on success criteria.
- **Pass**: Written research questions tied to business outcomes; shared definition of done.
- **Failure signals**: Skipping alignment  ->  wasted fieldwork; unfocused interviews.

## Anti-patterns

- **Market research as design research**: Segmenting buyers without observing post-purchase behavior.
- **Skipping fieldwork for blue-sky**: Refusing operational context interviews loses grounding.
- **Numbers without nuance**: Statistics on human behavior that overlook situational complexity.
- **Pain-point foraging**: Assuming users' struggles equal willingness to adopt your fix.
- **"What do you want?" research**: Asking users to invent the product instead of understanding their world.
- **Persuasion disguised as research**: Showing prototypes users will inevitably praise to justify decisions already made.
- **Innate product intuition myth**: Believing insiders can substitute for studying diverse users.

## Examples

- **Understanding The Problem**: A client commissioned an entry-level video-editing product based on market research showing a large untapped Windows audience with digital cameras. interviewed a dozen target users at home. Parents were the most motivated videographers—but only one of twelve had successfully connected camera to PC. Four days of qualitative research led the client to shelve the product, likely saving millions.
- **Research Question Alignment**: A client wanted to add premium features to hard hats. Interviews about workers' daily experience revealed social awkwardness wearing conspicuous safety gear off-site—not functional gaps. The team stopped development on enhanced functionality and declined a fashionable hard hat outside their competencies. Research informed a**no-go**decision, saving resources.
