## Decision frameworks

- **Defining Your UX Research Problem**: Translate business needs into specific, answerable research questions. - Use when scoping any study. - How: Stakeholder interviews → clarify objectives → write research questions before choosing methods.
- **How to Approach Desk Research**: Secondary research before primary fieldwork. - Use when entering unfamiliar domains. - How: Literature, competitor audits, analytics review, internal data mining.
- **Conducting an Effective Stakeholder Interview**: 6–8 one-on-one conversations to align objectives and build collaboration. - Use at project kickoff. - How: Ask about beliefs, burning questions, constraints, and how findings will be used.
- **Identifying the User Groups for Your UX Research**: Segment by behavior and context, not demographics alone. - Use when defining recruitment criteria. - How: Map user groups to research questions; prioritize highest-value segments.
- **Writing the Perfect Participant Screener**: Questions that filter for relevant context without leading. - Use before recruitment. - How: Behavioral criteria; screen out without revealing study hypothesis.
- **Arguments Against a Representative Sample**: Iterative design with theoretical sampling beats demographic balance for design research. - Use when stakeholders demand statistically representative samples. - How: Explain saturation, iterative rounds, and representative research vs. representative sample.
- **How to Find More Usability Problems with Fewer Participants**: The "magic number five" is a myth—structure tasks to maximize discovery. - Use when planning usability test scope. - How: Focus on key tasks; iterate rounds; don't expect 85% problem coverage from five users.
- **Deciding on Your First Research Activity with Users**: Usability testing as gateway when organization is new to UX research. - Use when building research culture from zero. - How: Start with usability test to demonstrate value; expand to field research once appetite exists.
- **Deciding on Your First Research Activity with Users**: Usability testing as organizational gateway when research culture is immature. - Use when UX research has zero credibility. - How: Plan test that stakeholders can observe; deliver prioritized fixes within one sprint.
- **Six-Step Research Process**:
- **Problem Statement Formula**: Topic + goal verb ("describe," "evaluate," "identify")—avoid open-ended "understand" or "explore."
- **Approach Selection Matrix**(Fig 3.1): Topic (organization, users, product, competition) × question type (generative, descriptive, evaluative, analytic) → method (interviews, contextual inquiry, usability testing, SWOT, heuristic analysis, A/B testing, literature review).
- **Recruiting Framework**: The net = screener; the bait = incentive; behaviors matter most.
- **Screener Design**(Perfetti): Screen for behaviors, tool knowledge/access, domain knowledge; keep short; follow up by phone for in-person tests.
- **Analysis Session Structure**(8 steps): Summarize goals → describe participants → describe data gathering → describe analysis types → pull quotes/observations → group into themes → summarize findings → document.
- **Analysis Ground Rules**: Focus on user context; respect structure; separate observations from interpretations; no solutions during analysis.
- **Finding Participants**: Repeatable recruiting via criteria-defined samples, panels, agencies—not friends-and-family guerrilla grabs. - Use when scoping any interview study. - How: Define behavioral criteria; use ResearchOps infrastructure or panels; avoid intercept recruiting for core studies.
- **Scheduling Interviews**: Tools and protocols that protect PII and reduce coordination friction. - Use once participants are identified. - How: Calendly-style scheduling; buffer travel time; confirm 24 hours ahead.
- **Incentives**: Thank-you practices that respect participants without conflating payment with consent. - Use for every session. - How: Match incentive to effort and locale; separate from NDA/consent; brand-building thank-yous.
- **The Interview Guide**: Structured flow—warm-up, core topics, wrap-up—without scripting answers. - Use before fieldwork. - How: Write guide from research questions; review with team; treat as flexible roadmap not teleprompter.
- **ResearchOps**: Infrastructure scaling research logistics across organizations—recruitment, legal, data governance, knowledge management. - Use in organizations running many studies. - How: Standardize protocols; automate scheduling/payment; library of approved consent forms.

## Checklists

1. Define the research problem and questions before choosing methods.
2. Stakeholder interviews align objectives and build collaborative ownership.
3. Screen for behavioral relevance, not demographic representativeness alone.
4. Iterative rounds with small samples beat one large balanced sample for design.
5. Usability testing is often the best first activity in research-immature organizations.
1. Base problem statements on outcome verbs so you know when you're finished.
2. Embrace recruiting as painful but essential—get good at it and everything speeds up.
3. Get the whole team into analysis; shared understanding beats circulated reports.
4. Document briefly even when moving fast—future you will thank present you.
5. Research is iterative; once you start, designing without it feels wrong.
1. Extensive prep—recruiting, scheduling, incentives, guides—before fieldwork begins.
2. Define sample criteria by behavior and context, not convenience.
3. Separate consent, NDA, and incentive into distinct clear processes.
4. Interview guides structure sessions without scripting participant answers.
5. ResearchOps scales logistics so researchers focus on interviewing quality.

## Heuristics

- **Research question anatomy**: Specific, behavioral, falsifiable—not "do people like it?"
- **Theoretical sampling**: Deliberate participant selection to test hypotheses across iterations.
- **Saturation**: Stop when new interviews yield no new patterns—requires analysis to assess.
- **Face validity of usability testing**: Easy for teams to observe; gateway to deeper methods.
- **Simple isn't a feature**: Key tasks must be identified before testing "simplicity."
- Planning is**thinking**, not**documentation theater**.
- Wrong participants waste**everyone's time**—screener quality matters more than sample size.
- First research activity is a**culture probe**—tests organizational appetite, not just the product.
- **Good research participant**: Shares target concerns/goals; embodies key characteristics; articulates clearly; matches relevant technology familiarity.
- **Semi-structured interview**: Prepared topics, not rigid script—flexibility to follow unexpected leads.
- **Usability testing definition**: Directed interview while representative user attempts tasks with prototype or product.
- **Usability is necessary, not sufficient**: Won't give you vision, market success, or task prioritization.
- **No labs, no masters**: Test in the wild—distractions, glare, screaming children are features, not bugs.
- **Literature review**: Pew Internet & American Life Project and similar sources when direct user access is limited.
- **Data types to extract**: Goals, priorities, tasks, motivators, barriers, habits, relationships, tools, environment.
- **Outliers**: Set aside participants who don't match target; don't accommodate Dan the political arguer who hates museums.
- **Research kit / country doctor's bag**: Pre-assembled tools and checklist for grab-and-go studies.
- **Naming convention**: `Study-SubjectName-Year-Month-Day`—obvious until you're in the throes of discovery.
- **Redundancy**: Backup drives; upload to shared storage immediately—plonk, iPhone in toilet, data gone forever.
- **Analysis as party for your brain**: Social creatures + pattern recognition machines = clarity emerges through conversation.
- **Sticky notes on whiteboard > lengthy report**: Visible artifacts beat ignored documents.
- **Sample criteria**: Behavioral relevance beats convenience—wrong participants waste everyone's time.
- **Saturation**: You don't know if you're done until analysis; plan buffer participants if timelines allow.
- **Legal and ethics**: NDA, consent, and incentive are separate documents with separate purposes.
- **Interview guide flow**: Corresponds to interview stages—introduction through wrap-up.
- **Prep like painting**: Tape, drop cloths, and supplies before the first brushstroke.
- Logistics are**infrastructure**, not**administration**—bad recruiting invalidates good interviewing.
- ResearchOps is**road maintenance**enabling a million commutes, not one driver's trip.
- The interview guide is a**map**, not a**script**.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Research Operations Scale
- **When**: Research volume exceeds individual researcher logistics capacity.
- **Process**: Document protocols  ->  automate incentives  ->  centralize consent templates  ->  standardize scheduling.
- **Pass**: Repeatable recruitment and documentation at organizational scale.
- **Failure signals**: Ad hoc logistics  ->  delays; inconsistent consent handling.

## Anti-patterns

- **Method-first planning**: "We need a usability test" before defining the question.
- **Friends-and-family recruitment**: Convenience samples that flatter designs.
- **Demographic balancing obsession**: Exploding sample size without improving insight quality.
- **Skipping stakeholder interviews**: Research that answers questions nobody cares about.
- **Bad recruiting**: Wrong people make any question useless; mismatched participants drift, tangent, critique link colors.
- **Professional research participants**: Vague screeners attract dissemblers chasing $25 gift cards.
- **Skipping analysis time**: Forging ahead to solutions without digestion wastes later effort.
- **Solutions during analysis**: Proposing fixes before insights are clear contaminates pattern-finding.
- **Usability lab obsession**: Illusion of control when you need wild unpredictability.
- **Testing right before launch**: Second most expensive testing; most expensive = customers doing it via support tickets.
- **Friends-and-family samples**: Flattering but unrepresentative of target behavior.
- **Consent form as NDA**: Legal intimidation suppressing open answers.
- **Rigid script reading**: Missing follow-up probes because the guide says move on.
- **No incentive clarity**: Participants unsure what they're agreeing to.

## Examples

- **Planning Ux Research**: David plans a usability test for a new checkout flow. Stakeholders want 20 demographically balanced participants. He whiteboards combinatorics showing balanced age/gender/region/experience requires 80+ users—impractical for design research. He proposes three iterative rounds of five participants each with theoretical sampling, focusing on key tasks identified from stakeholder interviews.
- **Research Process**: Museum project problem statement: "We will describe how parents of school-age children select and plan weekend activities by conducting telephone interviews and compiling the results." Recruiting screener asks activity frequency (movies, hiking, museums) without revealing study topic. Analysis session groups observations like "week rushes by, wake Saturday with no plans" into theme "needs reminders for organized activities" → design mandate: offer exhibit reminder sign-ups.
