# Content Design

## Decision frameworks

- **scan-first layout Principles**( scanning checklist):
- **Five-Second Test**: Show page briefly; can users identify purpose and key elements? - When to use: Early design validation. - How: Flash page five seconds; ask what it is and what they can do.
- **Four Critical Questions**(Home page must answer immediately):
- **Home Page Trade-off Acceptance**: Even best designs can't serve all constituencies—prioritize clarity over pleasing every stakeholder. - When to use: When turf battles erupt over "above the fold" promos. - How: Defend clarity; rotate promos; use cross-linking instead of Home clutter.
- **Navigation Handoff Rule**: Home may use different nav layout (e.g., vertical vs. horizontal) but must feel like same site when users leave Home. - When to use: Home page unique layouts. - How: Same sections, same labels, same Site ID—only layout may differ.
- **Scanning, Satisficing, and Muddling Through**: The three truths of Web behavior.
- **Scanning**: Users glance at headings, bold text, and links—like billboards at 60 mph.
- **Satisficing**: First reasonable option chosen, not the best after comparison.
- **Muddling through**: Using something without understanding how it works; "good enough" if it works. - When to use: Every design decision—especially navigation and page structure. - How: Support fast pattern-matching; make first options good; don't require full comprehension.
- **Billboard at 60 MPH Metaphor**: Pages are glimpsed, not studied. - When to use: Layout and visual hierarchy decisions. - How: Design for scanning (leads to Ch 49).

## Heuristics

- **Happy talk**: Content-free introductory blather.
- **Instructions**: Evidence users can't figure it out without help—fix design instead.
- **Noise**: Words that compete with actionable content.
- **Concision**: Shorter pages feel easier even when same information exists elsewhere.
- Think of words as**cognitive cost**—each sentence taxes scanners.
- Use concise copy principle's**double-halving**as aggressive editing discipline, not literal math.
- Treat instructions as**design bug reports**—remove words by fixing UI.
- **Visual hierarchy**: Size, color, position signal importance.
- **Conventions**: Underlined/colored links, logo upper-left, search box top-right.
- **Defined areas**: Distinct zones users can parse at a glance.
- **Clickability cues**: Buttons look like buttons; links look like links.
- **Noise**: Background patterns, excessive decoration, competing elements.
- **Tagline**: Good pairing with Site ID clarifies purpose.
- Think of each page as a**highway billboard**—one clear message, instant parse.
- Use**squint test**as cheap usability evaluation.
- Treat conventions as**free cognitive load reduction**—innovate only when yours is clearly better.
- **Above the fold**: Prime viewport—fiercely contested promo space.
- **Site identity and mission**: What site is and does—tagline essential.
- **Content promos**: Teasers to interior content—use sparingly.
- **Shortcuts**: Frequent tasks linked directly from Home.
- **Registration/login**: Entry points for returning and new users.
- **Too many cooks**: Everyone wants Home page space.
- Think of Home page as**waterfront property**—everyone wants a line on it.
- Use four questions as**Home page scorecard**—five-second test application.
- Treat promo success as**trap**—what works gets more promos until Home fails.
- **Scan/skim**: Rapid visual search for relevant words and shapes.
- **Satisficing**(Herbert Simon): Choosing what suffices rather than optimizing.
- **Muddling through**: Trial-and-error use without mental models.
- **Happy talk**: Introductory blah-blah users skip.
- **Instructions**: Rarely read; design should eliminate need for them.
- Think of users as**driving past your page**, not sitting down with tea to read it.
- Use satisficing as**design permission**—make the first visible option the right one.
- Assume users**won't learn your system**—they'll hack a path that works today.
- **Usability**: Making sites easy for people to use—less frustration, more satisfaction, return visits.
- **Self-evident**: Obvious at a glance without effort.
- **Self-explanatory**: Requires a little thought but rewards with understanding.
- **Thought balloons**: a useful metaphor for what users mentally say scanning a page.
- **Convention**: Familiar patterns users already understand (e.g., search box placement).
- Use cognitive effort minimization principle as**single rule in your head**if you only remember one thing.
- Think of question marks as**cumulative friction**—users won't leave immediately but goodwill erodes.
- Treat the**Back button**as user's emergency exit when thinking gets exhausting.
- **Information scent**: Clear cues that a path leads toward the goal.
- **Ambiguous categories**: "Tools? Or Lawn and Garden?"—cognitive cost per click.
- **20 Questions**: Each question splits solution space in half—model for navigation.
- **Trade-off**: Fewer clicks with harder choices vs. more clicks with easier choices—latter wins.
- Think of navigation as**a series of easy exams**, not one hard exam.
- Use**20 Questions**as IA test—does each choice eliminate roughly half the remaining confusion?
- Treat "too many clicks" complaints as**symptoms of ambiguous choices**, not depth.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Cognitive Effort Minimization
- **When**: Evaluating any page, label, link, or interaction.
- **Process**: Ask if an uninterested visitor grasps purpose and next action without effort. Eliminate question marks from ambiguous labels, hidden navigation, format surprises.
- **Pass**: Self-evident or self-explanatory at first glance; zero puzzlement.
- **Failure signals**: Clever naming; mystery navigation; instructions compensating for bad layout.

### Scan First Layout
- **When**: Designing pages for rapid scanning, not careful reading.
- **Process**: Use obvious headings, visual hierarchy, short paragraphs, meaningful labels. Design as if users are driving past a billboard.
- **Pass**: Key point understood in a quick scan.
- **Failure signals**: Wall of text; undifferentiated visual weight; buried calls to action.

### Unambiguous Choice Design
- **When**: Users must pick among options without re-reading or guessing.
- **Process**: Make options mutually exclusive and clearly labeled; avoid clever or overlapping categories.
- **Pass**: User picks without hesitation or re-reading.
- **Failure signals**: Overlapping categories; jargon labels; too many similar options.

### Concise Copy Principle
- **When**: Web copy review; pages need trimming.
- **Process**: Remove happy talk and needless words  ->  halve remaining text  ->  halve again. Every word must earn its place.
- **Pass**: Half or less of original word count with meaning preserved.
- **Failure signals**: Welcome paragraphs; redundant instructions; marketing filler.

## Anti-patterns

- **Welcome mat paragraphs**: Site greeters users ignore.
- **Redundant instructions**: "Click here to search" next to a search box.
- **Marketing fluff on task pages**: Users came to do something, not read about your values.
- **Legal/HR voice**: Corporate mission on Home page when users need products.
- **Novel navigation for novelty's sake**: Users must relearn basics.
- **Flat visual hierarchy**: Everything same size/weight—nothing scannable.
- **Mystery meat**: Clickable things that don't look clickable.
- **Wall of text**: Unbroken paragraphs in Web context.
- **Clever but unclear headings**: Cute wordplay that fails five-second test.
- **Promotional overload**: Home becomes billboard collage; hierarchy lost.
- **Corporate mission statement**: Users don't care; wastes above-fold space.
- **Rotating flash intro**: Splash pages violate courtesy and cognitive effort minimization principle.
- **Nav discontinuity**: Leaving Home feels like different site.
- **Everything to everyone**: No prioritization for primary audiences.
- **Designing for reading**: Dense paragraphs users will skip.
- **Requiring comparison shopping**: Users won't evaluate all options systematically.
- **Expecting users to learn**: They muddle; your elegant model is irrelevant.
- **Hiding key paths**: If it's not scannable, it doesn't exist.
- **Clever naming**: "Jobs" vs. "Career opportunities" when users need zero thought.
- **Mystery meat navigation**: Links/icons whose purpose isn't obvious.
- **Format inconsistency**: Bookstore sites forcing users to choose search type before searching.
- **Over-relying on instructions**: If you need paragraphs to explain the page, redesign the page.
- **Broad shallow menus**: Many options, all slightly overlapping—each click is hard.
- **Cute category names**: Marketing language that doesn't match user mental models.
- **Hiding depth behind one hard choice**: Mega-menu with 40 undifferentiated links.
- **Three-click rule literalism**: Sacrificing clarity to shave clicks.

## Examples

- **Concise Web Copy**: Before: "Welcome to WidgetCo! We're passionate about delivering innovative widget solutions that empower our customers to achieve excellence in today's fast-paced business environment. To search our catalog, enter your search term in the box below and click the Search button." After concise copy principle: Search box with placeholder "Search widgets." Tagline near logo if needed: "Industrial widgets since 1982." Instructions eliminated because search box is self-evident (cognitive effort minimization principle).
- **Scan First Layout**: A shaving-cream billboard sequence (1930s) communicated one idea per glance— analogy for Web pages. Apply: Home page has prominent Site ID + tagline ("Online bookstore since 1995"), clear navigation zones, headings larger than body text, bullets for features, links underlined in convention color. Squint test: logo, nav, and primary promo remain distinguishable. Flawed hierarchy: heading spans columns confusingly—slows scanning to reading speed.
- **Designing Home Page**: successful retail Home pages: Does visitor know what site is (tagline)? Can they start a task (search, browse categories)? Is inventory hinted (featured sections)? competitors (value prop visible)?
- **How We Use The Web**: User needs a chainsaw at Sears. They scan big department signs (TOOLS vs. LAWN AND GARDEN), pick Tools, scan aisle signs (POWER TOOLS), walk aisles scanning shelves. They don't read a store manual or compare every chainsaw spec.
- **Cognitive Effort Minimization**: A friend mentions XYZ Corp is hiring. You visit their site. Good design: mental reactions while scanning read "OK, there's Careers, that's the job list, that's the one I want." Bad design: "Career opportunities or Job listings? Is that a menu or a link?" the neighbor test: your next-door neighbor who barely knows the Back button should identify the site's purpose from the Home page alone.
- **Unambiguous Choices**: Sears chainsaw shopper: TOOLS vs. LAWN AND GARDEN is mindless (tools-oriented store → Tools). Within Tools, POWER TOOLS vs. HAND TOOLS is mindless.
