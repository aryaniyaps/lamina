# Field Research

## Decision frameworks

- **Homework / Self-Documentation (Diary Studies)**: Pre-session activities priming introspection—logs, screenshots, journals. - Use when behavior is habitual and invisible to participants. - How: Assign before interview; review artifacts together during session.
- **Show-and-Tell**: Participants demonstrate tools, spaces, and workflows in their environment. - Use in contextual field interviews. - How: "Walk me through…" "Show me how you…" in situ, not verbal summary alone.
- **Card Sorting and Prioritization Exercises**: Structured activities revealing mental models and priorities. - Use when understanding categorization or feature priority. - How: Bring cards; ask think-aloud sorts; note disagreements and hesitations.
- **Concept Testing with Neutral Presentation**: Prototypes shown without biasing ownership language. - Use when testing early concepts. - How: Present neutrally—avoid "I've been working on this"; embrace their worldview.
- **Process Mapping Together**: Co-created diagrams of workflows during interview. - Use when understanding multi-step processes. - How: Draw together; let participant correct your assumptions visually.
- **Process Mapping Together**: Co-created workflow diagrams during interviews revealing steps users forget to mention verbally. - Use for multi-step business processes. - How: Sketch on paper; let participant correct; photograph final map as artifact.
- **Team Roles in the Field**: Moderator, note-taker, photographer, observer—never one person doing everything. - Use when planning multi-person field sessions. - How: Assign roles before arrival; debrief immediately after each session.
- **Stages of the Interview**: Introduction → warm-up → main topics → reflection → wrap-up. - Use to structure session pacing. - How: Match guide sections to stages; don't rush to core topics before rapport.
- **Kickoff Logistics**: Location confirmation, recording setup, consent, incentive explanation at session start. - Use at the beginning of every interview. - How: Follow consistent opening script; test equipment before deep questions.
- **Facilitating Meaningful Reflection**: Questions reveal mental models, not predictions—ask about price to understand value models, not to set price points. - Use when interpreting responses. - How: Ask all sorts of questions but understand how to interpret answers.
- **Managing Difficult Moments**: Silence, emotion, tangents, and technical failures. - Use when sessions go off-script. - How: Comfortable silence; acknowledge emotion; gently redirect; always debrief.
- **Facilitating Meaningful Reflection**: Ask price/value questions to reveal mental models, not to set price points. - Use when tempted to take participant predictions literally. - How: Frame as exploration of how they think about value today.

## Checklists

1. Combine interviewing with artifacts, activities, and co-creation methods.
2. Homework and diary studies prime participants for deeper introspection.
3. Show-and-tell in context beats verbal summaries from memory.
4. Present concepts neutrally—check your worldview at the door.
5. Methods are combinable—interviewing provides foundational listening and questioning skills.
1. Assign distinct field roles—moderate, note, observe, debrief separately.
2. Follow interview stages; build rapport before deep topics.
3. Facilitate reflection; interpret responses through analysis, not literally.
4. Manage silence, emotion, and tangents with deliberate technique.
5. Debrief immediately after each session while memory is fresh.

## Heuristics

- **Priming through homework**: Self-documentation makes participants more introspective before the session.
- **Verbal summary vs. demonstration**: "What's your process?" from memory differs from watching the process.
- **Neutral concept presentation**: Ownership language pressures positive feedback.
- **Artifacts as evidence**: Forms, screenshots, and samples ground abstract answers.
- **Combine methods**: Interviewing is foundational; contextual methods amplify it.
- Interviews are**excavations**—artifacts and activities are**tools**, not distractions.
- Homework creates**shared reference objects**for deeper conversation.
- Show-and-tell beats**tell-and-tell**for behavioral truth.
- **Goal is reflection, not fact extraction**: Analyze and synthesize later; don't expect literal truths in session.
- **Guide flow matches stage flow**: Introduction builds rapport before sensitive topics.
- **Observer debrief**: Team members who didn't lead should share impressions immediately.
- **Recording and consent**: Always explain recording; get explicit permission.
- **Field kit**: Batteries, backups, consent forms, incentives ready before arrival.
- Interviews are**conversations with structure**, not**surveys aloud**.
- Silence is a**probe**—resist filling every pause.
- Post-session debrief is**fresh memory capture**—do it before the next interview.
- **Decision filter**: Ask whether this finding changes a specific design or business decision—if not, dig deeper or stop.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Contextual Field Interview
- **When**: Understanding real behavior in natural environment.
- **Process**: Visit user's environment  ->  observe workflow  ->  ask about artifacts  ->  probe decision points  ->  document with notes.
- **Pass**: Behaviors and context captured; surprises explored.
- **Failure signals**: Over-scripted interviews; leading questions; no artifact observation.

## Anti-patterns

- **Biased prototype intro**: "I'm really excited to show you my design."
- **Homework without follow-up**: Assigning logs but never discussing them in session.
- **Leading card sort categories**: Pre-labeled buckets that constrain mental models.
- **Moderator as sole notetaker**: Quality degrades on both facilitation and capture.
- **Rushing past warm-up**: Core questions before rapport yields shallow answers.
- **Taking predictions literally**: "Would you pay $X?" as pricing data instead of value mental model probe.
- **Skipping debrief**: Sessions blur together; insights lost.
