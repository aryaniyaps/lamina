## Decision frameworks

- **Stakeholder Definition**(SRI, 1963): "Those groups without whose support the organization would cease to exist"—include anyone without whose support your project fails.
- **Stakeholder Categories**: Executives, managers, subject matter experts, staff in various roles, investors/board members.
- **WWIC**(Paul Ford): "Why wasn't I consulted?"—fundamental question of the web; inquiry before work begins is peerless prophylactic against late objections.
- **Stakeholder Interview Flow**: Introduce purpose → explain confidentiality/aggregation → ask questions conversationally → restate how info will be used → verify ongoing participation expectations.
- **Business Requirements Criteria**: Cohesive, complete, consistent, current, unambiguous, feasible, concise (2–3 pages max).
- **Business Requirements Contents**: Problem statement, goals, success metrics, completion criteria, scope (in and explicitly out), risks/concerns/contingency plans, verbatim quotes, workflow diagrams.
- **Stakeholder Analysis Documentation**: Attitude toward project, stated goals, incentive alignment, influence level, communication network, required participation level, harmony/conflict with other interviews.
- **Test-Don't-Argue Response**: When boss imposes bad UX, say "Great idea—we can test that" and run a quick usability test. - When to use: Splash pages, extra form fields, animation, unconventional nav, etc. - How: Recruit 3 users; include boss's feature in tasks; let struggles speak; debrief with observers including boss if possible.
- **Common Boss Mandates Playbook**: - More personal information → test whether users abandon forms. - More pizazz (splash, animation) → test whether users skip or fail tasks. - Lowest-common-denominator design → test whether simplicity actually confuses. - When to use: Recognizing recurring organizational anti-patterns. - How: Prepare tasks that expose the mandate's cost.
- **Hiring a User Experience Leader**: Start at the top—first UX hire should be the right leader who builds competency, not a junior executor. - Use when organizations bootstrap UX teams. - How: Look for strategic vision, cross-functional credibility, and hiring ability—not just portfolio polish.
- **A Tool for Assessing and Developing the Technical Skills of UX Practitioners**: Competency matrix across research methods, analysis, and communication. - Use for self-assessment and team development. - How: Rate skills; identify gaps; plan deliberate practice.
- **Going Beyond Technical Skills: What Makes a Great UX Researcher?**: Curiosity, skepticism, communication, empathy, business acumen. - Use when hiring or developing researchers. - How: Evaluate soft skills equally with method proficiency.
- **How to Wow People with Your UX Research Portfolio**: Show process, impact, and thinking—not just deliverable screenshots. - Use when job-seeking or building credibility. - How: Case studies with problem, method, finding, impact, and reflection.
- **A Week-by-Week Guide to Your First Month in a UX Research Role**: Onboarding playbook—stakeholders, quick win study, debrief, process embedding. - Use when starting a new research role. - How: Week 1 stakeholders; week 2 desk research; week 3 first study; week 4 debrief and plan.
- **The Reflective UX Researcher**: Continuous learning through structured reflection on methods, biases, and outcomes. - Use throughout career. - How: Post-project retrospectives; journal what worked; seek critique.
- **The Reflective UX Researcher**: Post-project retrospectives on methods, biases, and stakeholder dynamics. - Use after every significant study. - How: Journal what worked; seek peer review of moderation; update personal competency matrix.

3. Document scope boundaries explicitly; scope creep kills functional projects.
4. Business requirements connect design to organizational success—no requirements = no way to know if you succeeded.
5. Organizational habits are as design-relevant as user behaviors.

3. Three users often suffice to show major harm.
4. Include observers so evidence is firsthand.
5. Common mandates: splash pages, extra fields, pizazz—all testable.
6. Avoid religious wars—use user behavior as arbiter.

2. Great researchers combine technical skills with curiosity, skepticism, and communication.

## Heuristics

- **Organizational terrain metaphor**: Startups = islands (quick view); corporations = Australia (impossible to see whole landscape, many dangers).
- **Observer effect as positive force**: Asking hard questions forces reflection and reveals motivation gaps across roles.
- **Neutralizing politics**: Identify opponents early; sell value in terms that matter to them.
- **Mule maxim**: The more important a project is to an organization, the more successful it will be.
- **Workflow**: Set of processes through which complex work gets done—diagram current and proposed states.
- **Internal users**: Stakeholders may themselves be users of back-end or admin systems.
- **Just enough stakeholder interviews**: Stop when you know stakeholders, roles, influence, risks, workflow changes, resources, requirements, and shared success definition.
- **Stakeholders = people who could put a sharp stick in your back**: Not fear them—get them on your side.
- **Inquiry is flattery**: Inviting participation empowers; never underestimate one obscure individual's ability to derail you.
- **Customer service as overlooked experts**: Respect + conversation = goldmine of user language and unmet needs.
- **Scope as boundary**: "Not touching the logo" and "not changing registration" written down prevents scope creep wars.
- **Startup enemy = momentum over reflection**: Counter with risk-ranked assumption testing (Design Staff / Google Ventures).
- **Splash page**: Intro users click through—almost always wastes goodwill.
- **Animation/Flash**: Motion that delays task completion.
- **Data hoarding**: Registration fields beyond user willingness.
- **Design by committee**: Compromise producing mush.
- **Religious debates**: Arguments without user evidence.
- Think of usability test as**diplomatic tool**—not "I told you so" but shared observation.
- Use boss's enthusiasm for their idea as**open door**to test it.
- Treat**25 Home pages**heuristic—patterns repeat; borrow tested arguments.
- **Job title confusion**: UX Designer, Researcher, Architect—organizations without clarity signal immature practice.
- **T-shaped skills**: Deep research expertise plus breadth across design and business.
- **Portfolio as proof of impact**: Decision changed, metric moved, team convinced.
- **First 30 days**: Relationship-building and quick wins establish credibility.
- **Reflective practice**: Researchers who don't examine their own bias repeat mistakes.
- Career growth is**impact trajectory**, not**method collection**.
- First month is a**trust auction**—quick wins buy permission for deeper work.
- Portfolio case studies tell**decision stories**, not**deliverable galleries**.
- **Decision filter**: Ask whether this finding changes a specific design or business decision—if not, dig deeper or stop.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Stakeholder Mapping
- **When**: Understanding internal stakeholders, politics, and constraints.
- **Process**: Identify stakeholders  ->  map interests and influence  ->  document constraints  ->  plan engagement.
- **Pass**: Stakeholder map with engagement plan.
- **Failure signals**: Ignoring silent veto holders; research without sponsor alignment.

## Anti-patterns

- **Pretending politics don't exist**: Turf wars you didn't know about will trample your work.
- **Manager sitting in on interviews**: Observer effect corrupts honest answers—hold ground.
- **Business requirements as design specs**: No specific solutions in requirements document.
- **Assuming client knowledge transfers to designer**: No mind meld—shared understanding requires inquiry.
- **Hostile witness takeover**: Remain calm, restate goal, ask open-ended question, or cut short if needed.
- **Skipping technical requirements hunters**: Locate everyone with specialized infrastructure knowledge.
- **Pure design argument**: Designers vs. marketing without user data.
- **Compliance without testing**: Building splash page because boss insisted.
- **Sabotaging test**: Biasing tasks or helping users prove boss right.
- **All-or-nothing refusal**: Burning political capital instead of testing.
- **Methods resume**: Listing every technique without showing outcomes.
- **Junior-first hiring**: Building team bottom-up without leadership vision.
- **Ivory tower research**: Expertise without stakeholder relationships.
- **Skipping reflection**: Repeating moderation mistakes across projects.

## Examples

- **Organizational Research**: Fantastic Science Center redesign: Marketing director knows visitor behavior assumptions customer service contradicts—reveals organizational disconnect + unmet visitor needs. Workflow diagram (Fig 4.1) maps VP Marketing → brief → writer drafts copy → designer collaborates → producer pushes live—exposes approval bottlenecks. - **Stakeholder Pressure**: Marketing manager wants splash page with animated brand story before Home. Response: "Let's test both versions Thursday." Three users on each: splash version—users annoyed, click "Skip" without reading, some miss navigation. Direct Home—users start tasks immediately. Boss watches session 2 user say "I don't have time for this." Data ends debate without designer being the villain.
- **Building Ux Career**: A VP hires their first UX lead from a muddle of job titles. advises: prioritize someone who has built a team, influenced product roadmaps, and can explain UX ROI to executives—not the strongest individual contributor. The new lead's first month: stakeholder interviews (week 1), desk research and screener (week 2), five-user usability test on checkout (week 3), debrief with video clips and assigned fixes (week 4). Portfolio case study documents 12% conversion lift.
