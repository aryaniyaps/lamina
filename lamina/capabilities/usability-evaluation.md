# Usability Evaluation

## When to load

- running usability tests
- quick prototype validation
- ongoing monthly testing cadence
- prototype available
- limited time
- need quick feedback
- ongoing product improvement
- no formal lab

## Decision frameworks

- **Heuristic Analysis**(Nielsen & Molich, 1990): 2–3 evaluators individually score a site against usability principles—quick, cheap, no recruiting.
- **Nielsen's Ten Usability Heuristics**:
- **Usability Definition**(five components): Learnability, efficiency, memorability, errors, satisfaction.
- **Cheap Tests First, Expensive Tests Later**: Paper → office → field; general audience → specific; competitor → sketches → prototype → every stage.
- **Usability Test Plan Checklist**(usability.gov): Objectives, subject/state, methodology, participants/recruiting, procedure, tasks, usability goals, completion rate, error-free rate.
- **Facilitation Principles**: Personable, patient, warm up then observe without intervening; embrace uncomfortable silences; never let designer-developer lead users.
- **Documentation Hierarchy**: Audio (high value) > time-stamped photos + audio > video (editing overhead, storage cost); screen capture + audio often sufficient.
- **MailChimp Mobile Remote Test**: Participant hugs MacBook from behind so iSight captures phone interaction + audio.
- **Usability Testing Method**: One user, representative tasks, think-aloud, observer notes, debrief, fix top problems. - When to use: Anytime you have something to show—paper, prototype, or live site. - How: Recruit loosely; 3–4 users per round; test in conference room; prioritize fixes.
- **Monthly Morning Test Cadence**: One morning per month, 3 users, fix then retest. - When to use: Ongoing development teams. - How: Schedule standing test day; rotate what you test; debrief over lunch.
- **Think-Aloud Protocol**: User narrates what they're looking at, thinking, and doing. - When to use: During test session. - How: Prompt "keep talking"; don't help unless stuck catastrophically.

## Checklists

1. Run heuristic analysis early with colleagues—one hour, obvious issues, before recruiting users.
2. Always be recruiting; maintain participant database.
3. Separate facilitator and observer; audio record with consent.
4. Test tasks derived from persona scenarios; rank failure severity in advance.
5. Combine qual (why) and quant (what) after launch for complete picture.
1. Test with one user at a time doing real tasks.
2. Three users per round finds most serious problems.
3. Test early—paper and wireframes count.
4. Don't help users; encourage think-aloud.
5. Debrief immediately; fix top problems before next round.
6. Monthly cadence beats annual elaborate studies.
7. Anyone on team can observe; facilitator role is learnable.

## Heuristics

- **Usability as basic manners**: Thwarting intended use = broken glass offered to guests.
- **Usability necessary, not sufficient**: Won't provide vision, market success, or task priority.
- **Tasks not equal**: Identify deal-breaker tasks (shopping cart, finding address/hours for location marketing sites).
- **Participants are single-use fuel**: Can bring back for improvement checks but previous exposure taints first-use data.
- **Self-blame conditioning**: Users blame themselves for system failures—ask how they expected it to work.
- **Eye-tracking**: $5K+ systems; worthwhile mainly for populations who can't articulate attention focus.
- **Heuristic analysis limits**: Simplistic, evaluator-dependent, may miss contextual issues, not substitute for usability testing.
- **Guided journey of imagination**: Usability test = participant pretends to use fully realized app for personally meaningful scenario.
- **Conan O'Brien facilitator**: Warm like Conan, then dispassionately watch participant flail like Conan.
- **Usability review alongside business/design/technical review**: Not separate activity—habit of quality.
- **Most expensive test = customers via support tickets**: Second most = testing right before launch.
- **Deal-breaker vs nice-to-have failures**: Know which task failures matter most before testing.
- **Loose recruitment**: Roughly target audience beats perfect demographic match.
- **Representative tasks**: Real goals users would have—not "click every link."
- **Debrief meeting**: Team watches, notes problems, agrees top 3–10 fixes.
- **Rochester plan**: Fix problems before next test round.
- **Start early**: Test sketches and wireframes—not just finished sites.
- **DIY testing**: Anyone can facilitate; doesn't require lab or PhD.
- Think of testing as**reading the user's mind out loud**—not proving your design right.
- Use**3 users per round**as enough to find major issues (not statistical proof).
- Treat "launch in two weeks" + "usability test" as**fire alarm**—do quick test anyway.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Guerrilla Usability Test
- **When**: Any time you have a prototype and one morning free.
- **Process**: Recruit 3 users  ->  run tasks in quiet room  ->  observers take notes  ->  debrief same day  ->  fix top 3 problems.
- **Pass**: Top usability issues identified and assigned for fix.
- **Failure signals**: Unrealistic tasks; no observer debrief; fixes deferred indefinitely.

### Lightweight Usability Cadence
- **When**: Ongoing product improvement without heavy infrastructure.
- **Process**: Monthly cadence  ->  3 participants  ->  same room  ->  team observes  ->  test current build  ->  fix before next round.
- **Pass**: Issues found and fixed between rounds; team exposure to users.
- **Failure signals**: Cancelled sessions; fixes not implemented; same internal testers only.

## Anti-patterns

- **Designer facilitating own work**: Hints and leading questions creep in when creation is criticized.
- **Unclear tasks**: Can't test what isn't specified.
- **Usability lab as default**: Illusion of control; escape risk is not your problem.
- **Video obsession without editing capacity**: Cops proves video value = good editing = vast time.
- **Testing only at launch**: Huge problems discovered too late to fix easily.
- **Heuristic analysis as complete evaluative strategy**: Misses user-system relationship in context.
- **Deferring testing until launch**: Too late for cheap fixes.
- **Over-recruiting**: Perfect participants while ship date slips.
- **Helping users during test**: Invalidates results; watch struggle.
- **Focus groups instead of usability tests**: People say vs. do mismatch.
- **Big annual lab study only**: Issues found once a year, fixed never.

## Examples

- **Evaluative Research**: Fantastic Science Center ticket purchase test: Scenario—science-minded school friend visiting weekend; task = find and purchase tickets. Test plan tracks completion rate and error-free rate. Facilitator presents scenario without helping when participant can't locate weekend hours on mobile. Observer notes nonverbal frustration at buried address, verbatim quote "I assumed tickets would be on the homepage." MailChimp-style remote mobile setup captures thumb interactions.
- **Lightweight Usability Cadence**: Team schedules first Thursday monthly. Tests 3 users on checkout flow in conference room with screen share. Tasks: "Buy a gift for your nephew who likes LEGO." Observers note: users miss guest checkout, confuse shipping with billing. Lunch debrief: top 3 fixes by Friday.
