# Competitive Analysis

## Decision frameworks

- **Competition Definition**: Top market-share players + Google first-page results + anything solving the same problem or helping users avoid it + inertia, habit, sofa, mom, duct tape, etc.
- **SWOT Analysis**(Albert S. Humphrey): 2×2 grid—internal Strengths/Weaknesses, external Opportunities/Threats; UX amplifies strengths, exploits opportunities, mitigates weaknesses, counters threats.
- **Competitive Audit**: For each competitor touchpoint, answer positioning, target audience overlap, differentiators, attribute embodiment, need overlap, strengths/weaknesses, conventions and opportunities.
- **Brand Audit Questions**: (1) Desired/avoided attributes, (2) Value proposition communication, (3) Customer associations from ethnographic interviews.
- **Brand = reputation + identity signals**: For many interactive products, brand experience IS user experience; interface voice IS brand personality.
- **Logo Assessment**: List contexts where users encounter logo; compare competitors in same contexts; importance depends on market (athletic apparel vs web app vs mobile icon).
- **Usability-Testing the Competition**: Task-based testing on competitor sites reveals user-perspective strengths/weaknesses before you design.

## Checklists

1. Cast the net wide, then narrow by role you want in customer's life.
2. Run SWOT from organizational research, then map UX to each quadrant.
3. Audit all touchpoints—websites, apps, kiosks, social, third-party storefronts.
4. Brand for interactive products often lives entirely in the experience.
5. Test competitors early and often—cheap insight before you build.

## Heuristics

- **Switching cost**: Users must love you more than they hate change—hardest competitor is what they use now.
- **Attention as rarest resource**: Convert attention into habit unless selling one very expensive item to few people.
- **Knowledge as competitive advantage**: If you do competitive research and they don't, you win.
- **Name as most important brand element**: Unique, unambiguous, easy to spell/say; mint.com vs Geezeo/Buxfer/Wesabe trust comparison.
- **Competitive landscape velocity**: Fastest-moving research topic—new options and collapsing categories daily.
- **User research won't help competitive internals**: You only see outside; deeper digging and extrapolation required.
- **Jungle ecosystem**: Hostile, constantly changing—design for adaptation and survival, not wishful thinking.
- **Three competitive questions**: What matters to customers? How are we better at serving that need? How do we show customers we're the superior choice?
- **Apples to apples brand comparison**: Don't compare Starbright Cleaners to Apple.
- **Premium = less necessary to daily life = branding matters more**: Local dry cleaner needs a name; Pepsi needs a universe.

## Evaluation rubrics

### Competitive Landscape Audit
- **When**: Understanding market landscape and differentiation opportunities.
- **Process**: Identify competitors  ->  evaluate on dimensions  ->  capture patterns  ->  note gaps and opportunities.
- **Pass**: Structured comparison with actionable differentiation insights.
- **Failure signals**: Feature checklist only; no user perspective on alternatives.

## Anti-patterns

- **"No one is doing what we do"**: Wrong—everything competes for time, money, and attention.
- **Market-share list only**: Misses search results, substitutes, and behavioral competitors.
- **Wishful thinking about differentiation**: User-eye view of comparative strengths, not internal narrative.
- **Logo-mark is dead / logo always critical**: Right answer is "it depends" on encounter contexts.
- **Ignoring competitor usability**: Test theirs before putting pencil to paper.

## Examples

- **Competitive Research**: Fantastic Science Center SWOT: Strengths = reputation, excellent staff; Weaknesses = exhibit-focused internal resources vs online technology focus. Opportunities = community desire for family-friendly weekend activities, more dads planning Saturdays. Threats = competition for attention, schools cutting field trips. Competitive audit of peer science museums' mobile apps and event calendars reveals conventions to match or leapfrog.
